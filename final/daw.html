Key Terms

  • Tracks: Horizontal lanes used to hold audio data
  • Plugins: Software effects (EQ, Compression, Reverb, etc.) applied to tracks.
  • Automation: Pre-programmed changes to volume, panning, or effects over time.
  • Bouncing: The final process of exporting the project into a stereo audio file (MP3 or WAV).

Source: The Mixing Engineer's Handbook, by Bobby Owsinski (Hal Leonard Books, 2021).

What is a Digital Audio Workstation?

A Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) is the software platform used to record, edit, and mix audio. It replaces the large mixing consoles and tape machines of the past, putting all the necessary tools into a computer interface. Popular examples include Pro Tools, Logic Pro, Ableton Live, and FL Studio. In the BYUH studio, we mostly use Pro Tools, but my personal favorite is Logic Pro.

Using a DAW gives you the ability to manage tracks, apply plugins, and use the mixer for precise volume and panning control.
